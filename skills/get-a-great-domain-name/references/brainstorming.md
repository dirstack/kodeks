## Product name vs domain name

The product name is what you *call* it; the domain is *where people access it*. You want them to match as closely as possible — ideally an exact match. Sometimes the extension becomes part of the name (people say "Booking.com," not "Booking" — the domain *is* the brand).

**Brainstorm the product name and domain name at the same time.** The common, painful mistake is falling in love with a product name, then finding every reasonable domain is taken. Decide the name only once you've confirmed the domain is gettable.

## Descriptive vs brandable

Two broad categories; many names sit in between.

- **Descriptive** — tells you what it does (Startup.jobs, BetaList.com). The name does the marketing. Best when bootstrapping without a marketing budget, or when organic search is the main growth channel. Downside: hard/impossible to trademark (see `tools-and-resources.md`).
- **Brandable** — doesn't describe the product; creates a feeling/identity you build meaning into (Google, Yahoo, homerun.co for hiring). More distinctive and trademarkable, but harder to brainstorm and needs marketing to instill meaning.
- **In-between** — e.g. handle.horse: "handle" describes the service (watching for an available social handle), ".horse" is unrelated, making it distinct and memorable.

## Brainstorming descriptive names

The trick is to **go beyond the obvious**. List every keyword related to the product/industry — then keep going into the details.

Example (social network for photographers): start with photo, camera, snap → then dig into camera parts, techniques, film-processing stages, lighting terms → aperture, viewfinder, darkroom, ISO, focus, tripod, **glass**. (`glass.photo` is a real photographers' network — the niche/technical word was the gem.)

Sources of depth:
- **Industry glossaries** — a goldmine of non-obvious terms.
- **AI chatbots** (ChatGPT/Claude/Grok) — give a few seed keywords and ask them to keep going; great at surfacing terms you'd never think of.
- **Thesaurus / OneLook** — synonyms, and words that start/end with specific letters.

Once you have a word you like, two paths:
1. Pair it with a fitting TLD → `glass.photo`.
2. Keep `.com` by adding a **prefix/suffix** → getglass.com, glassapp.com, tryglass.com. Not all will be free, but you only need one.

## Brainstorming brandable names

You can't just list industry keywords. Use these:

- **Start with a feeling.** Write down what you want people to *feel* (playful/serious, elegant/scrappy, accessible/exclusive, technical/approachable). Brainstorm words/metaphors that evoke that feeling, even if unrelated to the industry. `homerun.co` captures the feeling of a great hire better than any hiring word.
- **Metaphors & analogies.** Ask "what is my product *like*?" Order/clarity → compass, lighthouse, blueprint. A central place a team returns to → Basecamp. Speed → launch, momentum. These face less competition than literal keywords, so they're more often available or cheaper on the secondary market.
- **Invent a word.** Made-up names work (Spotify, Etsy, Trello) and twists on real words feel familiar yet distinctive (Typefully, Todoist). Start with sounds you like; shorten, combine syllables, swap letters. It must be easy to say, spell, and pleasant to hear. Invented words are far more likely to be available.
- **Let AI help.** Prompt with the emotion/metaphor and ask for associations, synonyms, or invented words with a certain sound. Generate volume.

## The Two-Part Rule

> When you tell someone your domain, they should only need to remember **two parts**.

- `.com` is the default — people don't actively remember it, so it **doesn't count** as a part. → two real words + .com is fine: RoomAI.com (Room+AI), BetaList.com (Beta+List), Fastmail.com (Fast+Mail).
- **Prefix/suffix approach:** one dictionary word + get/use/join or app/HQ → GetHarvest.com, UseFathom.com, JoinHoney.com, PostmarkApp.com, StreamlineHQ.com. Downside: people may call the product by the full domain ("Postmark App"). If you go this route, check whether the bare word (Harvest.com) is for sale too — good to know who owns it and whether you can upgrade later.
- **Non-.com:** the TLD is *not* assumed, so it **eats one of the two slots**, leaving one word. Good: wip.co, startup.jobs, highscore.money, handle.horse. Bad: getwip.co (three parts — someone will swap .co for .io or drop the "get").
- **Exception — a familiar phrase is one chunk.** buildinpublic.com is three words + .com but reads as one concept to its audience, so it's really two parts. Memory stores chunks of meaning, not individual words.

**The test (use this as a filter, not a law):** could someone hear the name once and type it into a browser from memory? Yes → within two parts. If they'd ask you to repeat it → too many.

## Top-Level Domains (TLDs)

There are 1,500+ TLDs. You only need the categories:

- **Generic** (.com, .org, .net, .info, plus newer .xyz/.link/.domains) — open to anyone. Caution on newer ones: some have strings attached (e.g. .new must use HTTPS and let users create something) and some charge steep premium renewals on short/dictionary words (the author's `yo.xyz` cost ~$3,000/yr and rising).
- **Country-code (ccTLD)** (.us, .uk, .nl, .de, .co, .io, .ai…) — tied to a country; some require residency (trustee services exist but add risk). **SEO caveat:** search engines may assume you're targeting that country.
- **Restricted** (.gov, .edu, .jobs) — limited to specific groups; research requirements before committing.

**Which to use?** `.com` is still king — default, trusted, most memorable. If it's taken, look at what established players in the niche use — that tells you what the audience is comfortable with.
- `.co` — popular with startups, but a non-technical audience may type `.com` by mistake and land elsewhere.
- `.io` — common in tech; Google treats it as generic.
- `.ai` — technically Anguilla's ccTLD, now synonymous with AI; popular and pricey. Author's view: great short-term (AI gets extra attention), but may look dated long-term like the "e-" prefix or "digital" — plan for a possible rebrand. A single-word `.ai` is a fine way to start.

**Always check the renewal fee before buying** (see Workflow B in SKILL.md): use a registrar's domain-transfer lookup to see the registry's current renewal price.

## Domain hacks

A domain hack uses the dot *inside* the word so the TLD becomes part of the spelling: del.icio.us, bit.ly, instagr.am, betali.st.

How to find them:
- Use tools that auto-generate hacks for a word (Domainr is the best — type a word, see options like art.ist / cre.at across hundreds of TLDs, including synonyms).
- Work backwards: browse short ccTLDs that match common word endings (.st, .ly, .io, .al, .er) and check if your keywords end those letters.

**Trade-offs:** fun and memorable, but awkward to say aloud ("betali dot S-T… no, the dot goes *before* the S-T"), and they rely on ccTLDs (SEO/registration quirks). Treat a hack as a great budget starting point and plan to upgrade later (BetaList went betali.st → betalist.com).

## Search across all extensions

Manually checking 1,500+ TLDs is impractical — **bulk search**: enter one word, check availability across all extensions at once. This found handle.horse and followup.fish.

Recommended config (Namecheap Bulk Search / "beast mode"):
- Enter one or more words you like.
- Set price range from 0 to your max budget.
- Check **"Show Premiums"** (includes registered-but-listed-for-sale domains).
- Check **"Hide Unavailable"** to keep results clean.
- Leave **"Append Prefix and Suffix"** off (you're already scanning non-.com).
- Enable **all TLDs**.

When scanning results, favor **alliteration** (word and TLD share a first letter): handle.horse, followup.fish — repetition makes it stick (Coca-Cola, PayPal, TikTok).

## Get ideas from marketplaces & expired auctions

Even if you don't buy, browsing is a great idea engine:
- **Marketplaces** (Sedo, Afternic, Atom) — flip the process: instead of searching availability, browse what's already for sale within your budget. Surfaces patterns and variations (seeing `yay.cam` listed led to registering `yay.camera`).
- **Expired-domain auctions** (SnapNames, GoDaddy Auctions, Park.io, Dynadot, Sav) — browse by keyword/TLD/length. Most won't fit, but gems appear. See `acquiring.md` for the end-user pricing advantage in these auctions.
