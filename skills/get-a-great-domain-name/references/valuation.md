# Valuing a Domain Name

There's no formula. Unlike real estate, **every domain is one of a kind** — there's no comparable property next door, which is exactly what makes valuation an art. Even similar-seeming names (banana.co vs mango.co) can differ wildly based on demand, trends, and who else wants it. Nobody — not a broker, not an algorithm — can give an exact value. The best you can do is an educated guess from comparable sales. Pair with Workflow D in `SKILL.md`.

## Researching comparable sales (NameBio)

Use **NameBio** (historical sales). The specific domain probably isn't in the database, so think creatively about "similar" in two directions:

1. **Same extension, similar words.** For banana.co, search other single dictionary-word `.co` sales — mango.co, sunrise.co, rocket.co. The extension sets the price range. Match roughly the same **length and type** — this matters most as domains get shorter. For 2–3 character domains, **length is one of the biggest value drivers**, so compare against same-length domains on that TLD.
2. **Same word, different extensions.** Check banana.com / banana.io / banana.net. Not a direct comp (a .com almost always sells for more), but it shows how popular that *word* is relative to others.

Neither is perfect; together they triangulate a reasonable range.

## Build a general feel for pricing

- **Browse recently *closed* auctions** (SnapNames, GoDaddy Auctions, Dynadot) — actual sale prices, not list prices, give a realistic picture.
- **Follow industry news** (Domain Name Wire, Domain Investing) — they report notable sales; patterns emerge over time.
- **Read marketplace listings** — but remember **asking prices are not sale prices**; many never sell at list, or sell only after big negotiation.

## What drives the price

- **Extension** — .com commands a premium; then well-known ones like .co, .io, .ai.
- **Length & simplicity** — shorter is more valuable. One dictionary word > two-word combo > three-word phrase.
- **Trends** — "AI" carries a premium now (was "crypto"/"blockchain" before). Trends inflate prices, sometimes temporarily.
- **Number of potential buyers** — a generic word ("cloud") has many buyers across industries → higher price; niche terms have fewer.
- **Seller's situation** — someone paying renewals out of habit prices very differently from a professional investor who knows the value.

## Skip automated appraisal tools

EstiBot, GoDaddy Domain Appraisals, etc. are not useful — algorithms can't capture the context that drives price (who wants it, why, and how badly). Use comparable sales instead.

## The end-user advantage in expired auctions

People bidding on expired domains are mostly **investors** who must buy low to flip high, so they cap their bids to protect a margin. As an **end user** who'll actually *use* the domain, you don't need that margin — if it's worth $500 to your business, you can bid $500 while an investor caps at $200. That gap is a real edge. (The author bought bio.link for $940 in such an auction and later sold it for $30,000.)

## A good domain is an asset, not an expense

Most startup spend (ads, tools, contractors) loses value the moment you spend it. A good domain is different: if the project fails, a premium domain **holds value and often appreciates**.

- trademark.ai: bought for $7,704 (monthly installments), project never happened, later sold for **$39,000**.
- bio.link: $940 → $30,000.

This isn't about flipping. The point: spending *more* on a quality domain is often **less** risky than spending less on a forgettable one. The cheap domain is a sunk cost if things don't work out; the premium domain is an asset you can recover or profit from. Set a budget you're comfortable with, trust the comps, and don't get emotionally attached to a number.
